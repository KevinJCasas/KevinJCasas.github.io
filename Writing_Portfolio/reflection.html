"As a Writer" Essay

Dealing with Indecisiveness

I have never liked writing. For as long as I can remember, I have struggled with the entire writing process; none of it has ever been easy. The act of sitting down and trying to put my thoughts into words has always been a source of discomfort for me, and I’ve grown to associate that discomfort with fear. That overwhelming fear has led to many hours of anxiety and frustration over writing assignments that shouldn’t elicit as much anxiety in me as they do. I’m already a terrible procrastinator, but because of the fear that I have for writing, that procrastination is multiplied as I try to stave off the negative feelings by leaving the task for as long as possible. Over the course of the semester, I have come to understand that a large source of the fear I have for writing comes from my indecisiveness. I am unable to make quick decisions or choose between options without taking the time to think through a variety of possible outcomes.

My thought process constantly gets in the way of my writing, and it shows in my writing process. Being put on the spot to write something is when this issue comes to light. The immediacy and influx of thoughts paralyzes my mind, and this is apparent in the very first writing exercise we did in class. When asked to write about a story that has stuck with me, more than one idea popped into my head. I wasn’t sure which one to choose, which one was the right story, which one would be the best example of myself that I would like to share. I ended up writing a short paragraph in class, trying to get something written in case I was called on. I continued to add and edit the paragraph after I had more time to think about what I wanted to say, and ended up with something that I felt okay with sharing. I took an entire week to decide on what I wanted to say, which shows how long it takes for me to feel comfortable with certain ideas.

In order to sort through all the ideas I have on a particular topic, I found that talking through my thoughts with others helps with this process. I tried this approach when I had to write the second chapter for my Fiction Writing workshop. I had been talking to a friend for two weeks about possible ideas for the novel, trying to figure out the direction I wanted to take with the story in the next chapter. With their help, I wrote down a multitude of notes in a Google document as we talked. We talked about the characters, the plot, the worldbuilding, and I was able to evaluate my ideas and make a decision for what the chapter should be about. The planning process consisted of me asking what they thought about certain plot points and getting their feedback to help me make a more informed decision, so I didn’t have to choose one path on my own. The conversation and feedback certainly helped me form an outline for the chapter.

When I sat down to write the chapter, though, I froze. I stared at the blank page on the screen and started to doubt my plan, unsure if the decisions I had made were the right ones. I wrote a couple of lines and immediately deleted them. I went back to planning, ruminating on all the ideas I had written down and trying to find a direction I was satisfied pursuing. It took two more days before I finally felt comfortable with an idea for the chapter and started writing. It wasn’t until I started to write that I began to understand what I needed to include the chapter. Much of what I ended up writing was new ideas that I hadn’t thought of before, and the words just seemed to flow.

After I submitted the chapter, I received a great amount of feedback from my peers through the in-class discussion we had and from their written critiques. There was praise, which certainly boosted my confidence, but more importantly the feedback they gave me to improve the chapter was invaluable. Some of the reviews mentioned that I should “include more humanizing details,” make “the dialogue more believable,” and “describe the [world] more thoroughly.” I had only received critiques and peer reviews a couple of times before this semester, but their feedback was helpful for me to reference in my rewrite of the chapter. A few weeks later, I submitted my revised chapter, and the feedback I received, then, was filled with even more praise and direct feedback on further adjustments I could make. The reviews focused more on “showing more than telling” and “build[ing] more tone into [my] characters.” It was such a new experience getting to write a chapter and receive a response to it with questions and things to fix, giving me more of an opportunity to take risks and try things out.

Receiving feedback has become a great discovery that has helped me get past my indecisiveness. The peer reviews that I received for my projects in this class were another example of how feedback helped me improve my writing. Without receiving the feedback that I did for each project, I would have been second-guessing my choices and changing what I thought was bad writing. I had my own bias towards my work, but my peers read my papers with fresh eyes and brought their own ideas about what I do to spruce up my writing. For my discourse analysis I was told to “give more examples” and “analyze the rhetorical section more thoroughly,” which I made sure to do for the final submission. The other peer reviews focused on more minor details, but were still useful in helping me make changes for the final submissions.

Most of this semester has been a journey in understanding the fear that I have for writing. I felt like I hit my stride as a writer in my Fiction Workshop class, this semester. I had an idea of a story that I was excited to make a reality and learned that I thrive off of feedback and talking out my ideas so I can form them into a more cohesive outline. My only goal as a writer these last four months was to become more comfortable with the act of writing and not find it as intimidating. I have done the most writing I’ve ever done this semester, and while I did struggle through the process, I feel like I created some good work that I’m proud of sharing. I know I still have a long way to go before I fully conquer my fear of writing, but I’m slowly starting to chip away at it and will continue to do so with more and more practice.
